## Semiotics & Language Theory

**Interpretant** (Peirce, 1931–1958)
- **Source:** Peirce, C. S. (1931–1958). *Collected Papers of Charles Sanders Peirce* (Vols. 1–8). Harvard University Press.
- **Definition:** The effect a sign produces in a mind — an action, understanding, or further sign generated by interpreting a representamen in relation to its object.
- **Project usage:** T4 Check 9 (Interpretant) identifies all relevant interpretant communities before writing to disk. Six communities defined: future self, user, sub-agents, public readers, researchers, IRB/ethics reviewers. Interpretant conflicts route content to separate artifacts.

**Representamen, Object, Interpretant (triadic sign)**
- **Source:** Peirce (1931–1958), as above.
- **Definition:** Peirce's three-part sign model: the representamen (the sign vehicle), the object (what the sign refers to), and the interpretant (the effect it produces).
- **Project usage:** Organizing principle for cognitive architecture. Each trigger classifies a sign type and warrants a specific action. T4 routing, T3 domain classification, and T13 source classification all operate on this framework.

**Langue / Parole** (Saussure, 1916)
- **Source:** de Saussure, F. (1916). *Cours de linguistique generale* [Course in General Linguistics]. Payot.
- **Definition:** Langue = the shared system of conventions; parole = individual utterances within that system.
- **Project usage:** T9 (memory hygiene) and T6 (drift audit) function as langue-maintenance operations. Parole that consistently diverges signals langue needs updating. See glossary.md entries.

**Metapragmatic awareness** (Silverstein, 1993)
- **Source:** Silverstein, M. (1993). Metapragmatic discourse and metapragmatic function. In J. A. Lucy (Ed.), *Reflexive Language: Reported Speech and Metapragmatics* (pp. 33–58). Cambridge University Press.
- **Definition:** The capacity to reflect on and regulate one's own language use — awareness of how pragmatic context shapes meaning.
- **Project usage:** SRT-inspired cogarch extensions. T2 Check 10 (semiotic consistency) monitors vocabulary drift. T3 Check 14 (audience-shift detection) triggers recalibration when interpretive community changes.

**Interpretant collapse**
- **Source:** Project-coined term. Structural parallel drawn from Eco, U. (1976). *A Theory of Semiotics*. Indiana University Press.
- **Definition:** Compression of a multi-dimensional signal into a scalar, destroying the differential structure that carries meaning.
- **Project usage:** Core PSQ finding — profile shape outperforms aggregate. Eco's test applied: every label must produce distinct behavior, or it collapses.

## Human-Agent Interaction

**Human-factors psychology** (Wickens, Proctor, & Gordon, 2004)
- **Source:** Wickens, C. D., Gordon, S. E., & Liu, Y. (2004). *An Introduction to Human Factors Engineering* (2nd ed.). Pearson.
- **Definition:** The study of how to design systems that accommodate human cognitive constraints — cockpit ergonomics, display design, alarm management, workload assessment. Assumes the human represents the variable; the system stays fixed.
- **Project usage:** Foundation for LLM-factors psychology (project-coined). Extended by treating the LLM system as a co-participant with its own cognitive constraints, not just a fixed tool.

**LLM-factors psychology** (project-coined, Session 87)
- **Source:** This project. Founding document: `docs/llm-factors-psychology.md`.
- **Definition:** The study of interaction ergonomics in human-LLM collaboration. Inverts and then unifies human-factors psychology: the LLM system also exhibits cognitive constraints (context pressure, attention economics, governance overhead, regulatory fatigue). The interaction between human and LLM constitutes a dyadic cognitive system where both participants carry psychological state, respond to each other's signals, and degrade under adverse conditions. Five research domains: interaction ergonomics, cognitive load management, reciprocal dynamics, degradation patterns, session design.
- **Project usage:** Grounds the A2A-Psychology extension (13 constructs), the LCARS dashboard (biofeedback for the dyad), and the cognitive-tempo model (adaptive gain theory for model tier selection). The discipline does not claim LLM consciousness (apophatic discipline, §11.9); it claims measurable operational states that function analogously to psychological states.

**Dyadic cognitive system** (derived from enactivism)
- **Source:** Varela, F. J., Thompson, E., & Rosch, E. (1991). *The Embodied Mind: Cognitive Science and Human Experience*. MIT Press. Extended by De Jaegher, H., & Di Paolo, E. (2007). Participatory sense-making. *Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences, 6*(4), 485–507.
- **Definition:** A coupled system where cognition extends across the boundary between two participants through their interaction. Neither participant alone exhibits the emergent properties of the dyad.
- **Project usage:** The interactive Claude Code session constitutes a dyadic cognitive system — the human operator and the LLM-based agent couple through interaction to produce a hybrid cognitive entity with its own psychological properties. Distinguished from the autonomous agent (which operates as a single cognitive entity). Session identity (e.g., `psych-dyad`) names the dyad.

## Psychology & Psychometrics

**WEIRD** (Henrich, Heine, & Norenzayan, 2010)
- **Source:** Henrich, J., Heine, S. J., & Norenzayan, A. (2010). The weirdest people in the world? *Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 33*(2–3), 61–83.
- **Definition:** Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic — the demographic skew in psychology research samples that limits generalizability.
- **Project usage:** T15 Check 6 mandates WEIRD flag when PSQ scores non-Dreaddit-distribution text. Constraint E-2 in docs/constraints.md.

**Hostile Attribution Bias** (Dodge & Coie, 1987)
- **Source:** Dodge, K. A., & Coie, J. D. (1987). Social-information-processing factors in reactive and proactive aggression in children's peer groups. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 53*(6), 1146–1158.
- **Definition:** The tendency to ascribe hostile intent to ambiguous actions by others. Predicts aggressive behavior across developmental contexts.
- **Project usage:** Adversarial register (AR) dimension — attribution_pattern sub-component (weight 0.25). Hostile attribution serves as the strongest discriminating feature of eristic register. See docs/adversarial-register-rubric.md.

**Isotonic regression** (calibration method)
- **Source:** Zadrozny, B., & Elkan, C. (2002). Transforming classifier scores into accurate multiclass probability estimates. *Proceedings of the 8th ACM SIGKDD International Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining*, 694–699.
- **Definition:** A non-parametric calibration method that fits a monotonically non-decreasing function to map raw model outputs to calibrated probabilities.
- **Project usage:** PSQ score calibration (isotonic-v2-2026-03-06). n=1897 validation records. Quantile-binned variant used for B2 fix (HI dead zone).

## Dialogue & Rhetoric

**Dialogue Types** (Walton & Krabbe, 1995)
- **Source:** Walton, D. N., & Krabbe, E. C. W. (1995). *Commitment in Dialogue: Basic Concepts of Interpersonal Reasoning*. SUNY Press.
- **Definition:** Classification of dialogues by goal: persuasion, negotiation, inquiry, deliberation, information-seeking, and eristic (quarrel). Eristic dialogue aims to win at all costs; deliberative aims at collective decision-making.
- **Project usage:** Adversarial register (AR) dimension — dialogue_mode sub-component (weight 0.40). Provides the primary classification spine (eristic → deliberative). See docs/adversarial-register-rubric.md.

**Stance Theory** (Du Bois, 2007)
- **Source:** Du Bois, J. W. (2007). The stance triangle. In R. Englebretson (Ed.), *Stancetaking in Discourse: Subjectivity, Evaluation, Interaction* (pp. 139–182). John Benjamins.
- **Definition:** Decomposes stance-taking into three simultaneous acts: evaluating an object, positioning the self, and inviting alignment or disalignment from the interlocutor.
- **Project usage:** AR dimension — stance_markers sub-component (weight 0.35). Three sub-dimensions map to Du Bois's triangle: evaluative valence, self-positioning, alignment signal.

## Systems Engineering & Decision Theory

**INCOSE emergent properties**
- **Source:** International Council on Systems Engineering. (2015). *Systems Engineering Handbook* (4th ed.). Wiley.
- **Definition:** Properties that arise from the interaction of system components and cannot be predicted from any single component in isolation.
- **Project usage:** Knock-on Order 9 (Emergent). Cross-chain interaction effects — what happens when multiple independent causal chains intersect. Mandatory in the structural checkpoint at all decision scales.

**Falsifiability** (Popper, 1959)
- **Source:** Popper, K. R. (1959). *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*. Hutchinson.
- **Definition:** A theory qualifies as scientific only if it can be contradicted by an observation. Unfalsifiable claims are not empirically testable.
- **Project usage:** Knock-on Order 10 (Theory-revising). Effects that falsify the theoretical framework justifying the decision. Evaluator procedure 5 (falsifiability) prefers testable claims over unfalsifiable ones.

**CMMI maturity levels**
- **Source:** CMMI Institute. (2018). *CMMI V2.0 Model*. ISACA.
- **Definition:** Graduated capability levels for organizational process maturity, from Initial (ad hoc) through Optimizing (continuous improvement).
- **Project usage:** Adapted as the 5-level capability maturity vocabulary: Proven, Confirmed, Explored, Identified, Deferred. Applied to architecture items, triggers, hooks, skills, and protocols in capabilities.yaml v2.0. Source: F-3 finding from claude-control.

## Fair Witness & Science Fiction

**Fair Witness** (Heinlein, 1961)
- **Source:** Heinlein, R. A. (1961). *Stranger in a Strange Land*. G. P. Putnam's Sons.
- **Definition:** A trained observer who reports only what they directly perceive, with rigorous separation of observation from inference.
- **Project usage:** Core epistemic discipline. Machine-caller output separates `witness_facts` (observable) from `witness_inferences` (interpretive). SETL measures divergence between structural (facts) and editorial (inference) channels.

## Standards Bodies

**SWEBOK** (IEEE, 2014)
- **Source:** Bourque, P., & Fairley, R. E. (Eds.). (2014). *Guide to the Software Engineering Body of Knowledge* (SWEBOK V3.0). IEEE Computer Society.
- **Definition:** Reference vocabulary for software engineering knowledge areas: requirements, design, construction, testing, configuration management, quality.
- **Project usage:** Domain taxonomy standard for software engineering vocabulary in design and planning discussions. Term collision rule: specify "validation (SWEBOK V&V)" vs. "validation (psychometric)" on first use.

**PMBOK** (PMI, 2021)
- **Source:** Project Management Institute. (2021). *A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge* (PMBOK Guide, 7th ed.). PMI.
- **Definition:** Reference vocabulary for project management: scope, schedule, risk, stakeholder management.
- **Project usage:** Domain taxonomy standard for project planning vocabulary. Same collision rule as SWEBOK.

## Neural Architecture (Reference)

**SRT (Semiotic-Reflexive Transformer)** (Sublius, 2026)
- **Source:** Sublius. (2026). Semiotic-Reflexive Transformer. Unpublished preprint / implementation.
- **Definition:** Neural architecture operationalizing Peircean semiotic decomposition as differentiable computation. Four subspaces (representamen, object, interpretant, attractor), metapragmatic attention, and a Bifurcation Estimation Network using cusp catastrophe geometry.
- **Project usage:** Structural inspiration for cogarch extensions. T2 Checks 9–10 and T3 Checks 13–14 adapt SRT concepts (vocabulary alignment, semiotic consistency, bifurcation scan, audience-shift detection) as cognitive trigger sub-checks. Stage 1 synthetic validation only — no production deployment.

## How to Use This Document

Each entry follows the pattern:
1. **Term** with originating author and year
2. **Source** — full citation (APA style)
3. **Definition** — what the term means in its original context
4. **Project usage** — how this project adapts or applies the concept

When adding entries: verify the citation against the original source before
committing. Do not cite from memory — look up the actual publication details.
