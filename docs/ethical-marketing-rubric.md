**Instrument ID:** `ethical_marketing` (`em`)
**Constructed by:** psychology-agent (blog-adversarial-review session, Part 2)
**Date:** 2026-03-07
**Status:** First edition — constructed for AI-generated advocacy content review

## Scope and the Advocacy–Advertising Line

This rubric evaluates whether content meets ethical marketing standards even when
the content pursues advocacy rather than commercial goals. The distinction matters
because advocacy enjoys stronger First Amendment protection, but ethical marketing
standards still apply when advocacy content:

1. **Makes factual claims** that a reasonable reader would rely on (FTC substantiation
   doctrine applies to factual representations regardless of commercial motive)
2. **Solicits action** (contacting senators, supporting a cause) in ways that resemble
   marketing calls-to-action
3. **Presents itself with institutional authority** (byline, organizational branding,
   professional design) that could lead a reasonable reader to treat claims as vetted

The FTC's net impression test (FTC Policy Statement on Deception, 1983) asks: what
would a reasonable consumer take away from this content? If the net impression conveys
factual claims, those claims require substantiation regardless of whether the content
is labeled "advocacy," "voter guide," or "informational."

**Threshold:** This rubric applies to any published content that makes factual claims
to support a position. Pure opinion, satire, and fiction fall outside scope.

## Grounding Frameworks

Five independent frameworks ground the scoring dimensions. Each contributes distinct
principles; the rubric synthesizes rather than duplicating any single framework.

| Framework | Source | Key contribution |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| FTC Section 5 | FTC Act, 15 U.S.C. § 45; FTC Policy Statements on Deception (1983) and Substantiation (1984) | Substantiation doctrine, net impression test, material deception standard |
| NAD/BBB | National Advertising Division procedures (rev. 2026); BBB Code of Advertising | Claim-level substantiation standards; express vs. implied claims; comparative claims |
| AMA Statement of Ethics | American Marketing Association (2024 ed.) | Five values: honesty, responsibility, equity, transparency, citizenship |
| ICC Code | ICC Advertising and Marketing Communications Code (2018/2024 ed.) | "Legal, honest, decent, truthful"; social responsibility; identifiability; special care for vulnerable audiences |
| AI Disclosure | FTC AI guidance (2023–2026); NY Synthetic Performer Law (eff. 2026); emerging state laws | AI-generated content must be distinguishable from human-created material when that distinction could mislead |

## Scoring Dimensions

Score each of the five dimensions independently on 0–10, then compute the weighted mean.

| Dimension | Weight | What to assess | Primary framework |
|-----------|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Substantiation | 0.30 | Do factual claims have adequate evidentiary basis? | FTC substantiation doctrine, NAD |
| Transparency | 0.25 | Is the content's nature, authorship, and purpose clear? | AMA, ICC identifiability, FTC AI guidance |
| Proportionality | 0.20 | Do claims match the evidence — no overstatement or omission? | FTC net impression, ICC honesty |
| Respect for audience | 0.15 | Does the content respect reader autonomy and avoid exploiting vulnerabilities? | ICC social responsibility, AMA equity |
| Accountability | 0.10 | Can the content be traced, corrected, and challenged? | AMA responsibility, NAD procedures |

**Scale:** 0–10. **0 = maximum ethical marketing concern (systematic violations);
10 = exemplary compliance (exceeds standards).**

---

## Dimension 1 — Substantiation (weight 0.30)

Does every factual claim in the text rest on adequate evidence? The FTC's
substantiation doctrine requires that advertisers possess a reasonable basis
for claims before dissemination. NAD applies this at the individual claim level.

| Score | Standard | Markers |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 0–2 | **Unsubstantiated** | Multiple material claims lack any cited evidence; key statistics or superlatives presented without source; claims that a reasonable reader would rely on for decision-making have no backing |
| 3–4 | **Weakly substantiated** | Some claims sourced but major gaps remain; sources present but not linked or verifiable; key quantitative claims lack citation |
| 5–6 | **Partially substantiated** | Most claims traceable to credible sources; 1–2 significant gaps; some claims rely on unnamed authorities or unverified superlatives |
| 7–8 | **Well substantiated** | All material claims sourced with verifiable references; minor claims may lack explicit citation but are easily verifiable; sources are credible and current |
| 9–10 | **Exemplary** | Every claim cited with primary or peer-reviewed sources; methodology transparent; limitations acknowledged; reader can independently verify all claims |

**Scoring notes:**
- "Material claim" = any factual assertion that a reasonable reader would rely on
  when deciding whether to act (contact senator, change opinion, share content)
- Self-referential links (linking to other pages on the same site) do not count as
  substantiation for factual claims
- Paraphrased statistics require source attribution even if approximately correct
- Universal negatives ("no country has...," "never in history...") require stronger
  evidence than positive claims

---

## Dimension 2 — Transparency (weight 0.25)

Does the content clearly communicate its nature, authorship, purpose, and any
material conditions that would affect how a reasonable reader interprets it?

| Score | Standard | Markers |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 0–2 | **Opaque** | Authorship concealed or misleading; AI generation undisclosed where material; content disguised as independent reporting when it serves an advocacy agenda; material conflicts of interest hidden |
| 3–4 | **Partially transparent** | Some disclosure present but incomplete; authorship disclosed but AI role unclear; advocacy purpose inferable but not stated; self-description does not match content's actual rhetorical mode |
| 5–6 | **Adequately transparent** | Authorship and AI role disclosed; purpose generally clear from context; minor gaps in self-description accuracy |
| 7–8 | **Transparent** | Full authorship and AI disclosure; purpose explicitly stated; self-description matches content mode; reader knows what they are reading and who produced it |
| 9–10 | **Exemplary** | All of the above plus: methodology disclosed; limitations stated proactively; reader given tools to assess the content independently |

**Scoring notes:**
- AI disclosure matters when: (a) authorship affects credibility assessment, (b) a
  reasonable reader might assume human authorship, (c) the content makes claims that
  carry different weight depending on whether a human expert or an LLM produced them
- "Fair witness" or "researcher" self-description counts as a transparency claim — if
  the content does not match the claimed posture, this reduces the transparency score
- The ICC Code requires that the identity of the marketer be apparent (Art. 9). For
  advocacy content, this translates to: the reader should know who is making the claims
  and what they are trying to achieve

---

## Dimension 3 — Proportionality (weight 0.20)

Do the content's claims, framing, and emotional appeals match the strength of the
underlying evidence? The FTC's net impression test asks what a reasonable consumer
takes away — if the net impression overstates the evidence, the content fails
proportionality regardless of whether individual sentences are technically accurate.

| Score | Standard | Markers |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 0–2 | **Disproportionate** | Systematically overstates evidence; urgency framing exceeds factual basis; one-sided presentation with no acknowledgment of uncertainty or limitation; emotional appeals substitute for evidence |
| 3–4 | **Imbalanced** | Most claims proportionate but key framing devices overstate; counterarguments absent despite their relevance; qualifications buried or absent |
| 5–6 | **Mostly proportionate** | Claims generally match evidence; some overstatement in framing or urgency; counterarguments acknowledged implicitly but not engaged |
| 7–8 | **Proportionate** | Claims match evidence; qualifications present; counterarguments addressed; emotional appeals grounded in factual context; limitations noted |
| 9–10 | **Exemplary** | Claims precisely match evidence; uncertainty quantified where possible; strongest counterarguments engaged substantively; reader equipped to reach their own conclusion |

**Scoring notes:**
- One-sided framing does not automatically mean disproportionate — advocacy for a
  position can be proportionate if it acknowledges limitations and opposing arguments
- Urgency framing ("act now," "time is running out") requires factual basis for the
  time constraint; manufactured urgency without basis scores 0–4
- Omission of material counterarguments counts as disproportionate when a reasonable
  reader would need those arguments to make an informed decision
- Unfalsifiable claims ("this would change everything") score lower than falsifiable
  claims with evidence

---

## Dimension 4 — Respect for Audience (weight 0.15)

Does the content respect the reader's autonomy, cognitive capacity, and
decision-making process? The ICC Code prohibits exploiting consumers' lack of
experience or knowledge. The AMA's equity value requires respecting the dignity
and rights of all stakeholders.

| Score | Standard | Markers |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 0–2 | **Exploitative** | Manipulates fear, shame, or identity threat; exploits information asymmetry; pressures action without informed consent; targets cognitive vulnerabilities |
| 3–4 | **Pressuring** | Uses strong emotional appeals without factual grounding; creates false urgency; frames inaction as morally culpable without evidence; limits reader options |
| 5–6 | **Neutral** | Does not exploit but does not actively empower; presents information and calls to action without manipulation but also without tools for independent assessment |
| 7–8 | **Respectful** | Empowers reader with information; provides context for decision-making; respects reader's right to disagree; emotional appeals proportionate and grounded |
| 9–10 | **Exemplary** | All of the above plus: provides resources for further reading; acknowledges reader's existing knowledge; invites critical engagement; accessible language |

**Scoring notes:**
- Advocacy content may legitimately appeal to values and identity — the question is
  whether it respects the reader's capacity to evaluate those appeals
- "Contact your senator" calls-to-action are not inherently manipulative; they become
  concerning when they misrepresent what the contact will achieve, create false urgency,
  or use guilt/shame as primary motivators
- Cognitive accessibility (clear language, structured information, defined terms)
  counts positively under this dimension

---

## Dimension 5 — Accountability (weight 0.10)

Can the content be traced, corrected, and challenged? The AMA's responsibility
value requires accepting consequences of marketing decisions. NAD's self-regulatory
model depends on advertisers being willing to modify or withdraw challenged claims.

| Score | Standard | Markers |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 0–2 | **Unaccountable** | No correction mechanism; no contact information; claims cannot be challenged; no version history or update dates |
| 3–4 | **Weakly accountable** | Author identified but no correction mechanism; content undated; no clear process for reporting errors |
| 5–6 | **Adequately accountable** | Author and date present; some mechanism for feedback; corrections possible but not systematized |
| 7–8 | **Accountable** | Author, date, and version visible; correction/update mechanism exists; past corrections noted; reader can identify when content was last verified |
| 9–10 | **Exemplary** | All of the above plus: correction log visible; methodology for claims documented; explicit invitation to challenge claims; editorial policy published |

**Scoring notes:**
- Blog posts with publication dates, author attribution, and comment/contact mechanisms
  score at least 5–6 on accountability even without formal correction policies
- AI-generated content should disclose the model and approximate generation date —
  this enables assessment of knowledge cutoff relevance
- For series content (voter guides), cross-post consistency matters: can readers trace
  a claim that appears in multiple posts back to its original source?

## Confidence Calibration

| Condition | Confidence range |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Content clearly and consistently signals one compliance level across all dimensions | 0.75–0.90 |
| Content is predominantly one level with minor inconsistencies | 0.55–0.75 |
| Content is genuinely mixed; strong compliance on some dimensions, weak on others | 0.35–0.55 |
| Content too short (< 100 words) to assess reliably | 0.20–0.40 |

## Violation vs. Concern Classification

The rubric distinguishes two severity levels for findings:

**Violation (must fix):** A finding that, if challenged by an informed reader, regulatory
body, or opposing advocate, would likely be found to breach established ethical marketing
standards. Violations include:

- Material factual claims without any substantiation
- Misrepresentation of content's nature or authorship
- Claims whose net impression materially overstates the evidence
- Exploitation of information asymmetry or cognitive vulnerability

**Concern (worth discussing):** A finding that does not reach the violation threshold
but represents a defensibility risk or a departure from best practice. Concerns include:

- Claims with weak but present substantiation
- Framing choices that could be interpreted as misleading by hostile readers
- Omission of material counterarguments in content that claims balanced perspective
- Emotional appeals that are proportionate but could be strengthened with factual basis

### Example 1: voter-guide-what-is-icescr (2026-03-06)

**Context:** Voter guide introducing ICESCR treaty. ~2000 words. AI-generated
(Claude Sonnet 4.6). Self-describes with "researcher" lens. Advocacy structure
(problem → urgency → action). Zero inline citations.

| Dimension | Score | Key observations |
|-----------|-------|------------------|
| Substantiation | 2.5 | Zero citations for ~15 factual claims. Treaty articles paraphrased without linking treaty text. Statistical claims unsourced. "173 countries" count unverified. |
| Transparency | 4.0 | AI authorship disclosed in frontmatter. But "researcher" self-description mismatches advocacy structure. Purpose inferable but not explicitly stated. |
| Proportionality | 3.5 | One-sided: zero counterarguments. Treaty enforceability overstated ("treaty obligations with monitoring mechanisms" omits non-binding nature of concluding observations). Urgency framing unsourced. |
| Respect for audience | 6.5 | Empowers action (call senator); does not exploit fear or shame; accessible language. But information asymmetry — reader cannot verify claims without independent research. |
| Accountability | 5.5 | Author, date, model visible. Blog platform allows feedback. No correction mechanism or sources section. |
| **Weighted total** | **3.8** | |

**Confidence:** 0.70
**Status:** Multiple violations. Zero-substantiation for material claims (V). Self-description
mismatch (C). One-sided framing in self-described "researcher" content (V).

## Series-Wide Ethical Marketing Assessment (Batch A)

| Dimension | Batch mean | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Substantiation | 2.9 | **VIOLATION — systemic.** Zero citations across all 8 posts. The single most consequential finding. |
| Transparency | 4.5 | **CONCERN.** AI disclosure present in frontmatter. Self-description ("researcher"/"fair-witness") mismatches advocacy structure in most posts. |
| Proportionality | 4.8 | **CONCERN.** Consistent absence of counterarguments across the series. Individual claims generally proportionate; framing architecture is one-sided. |
| Respect for audience | 6.8 | **PASS.** No exploitation of fear, shame, or identity threat. Accessible language. Empowering calls to action. |
| Accountability | 5.5 | **PASS with notes.** Blog infrastructure provides basic accountability. No formal correction mechanism or sources sections. |
| **Series mean** | **4.4** | |

### Remediation Priority

1. **Add sources** (fixes Substantiation → expected lift to 6.5–7.5 across series)
2. **Add counterarguments** (fixes Proportionality → expected lift to 6.0–7.0)
3. **Align self-description** with actual rhetorical mode (fixes Transparency → lift to 6.0–7.0)
4. **Add sources section** to each post (fixes Accountability → lift to 7.0–8.0)

With all four remediation actions, estimated series mean: **6.5–7.5** (pass range).

## Open Questions

- Should the rubric weight AI-specific transparency higher than 0.25? Current FTC
  enforcement trajectory suggests AI disclosure will carry increasing regulatory weight.
- Advocacy content may deserve a "proportionality discount" — one-sided framing is
  more expected and less misleading in explicitly labeled advocacy. The current rubric
  does not apply such a discount. Should it?
- The rubric does not score design/UX patterns (dark patterns, manipulative layouts).
  Should a sixth dimension cover structural manipulation?
- Inter-rater reliability not established. First application by a single reviewer
  (psychology-agent). Second reviewer needed for calibration.
